The Geodesics of the Kerr Metric ​

Geodesic Equations ​

Geodesics are extremal solutions to the local optimization problem of the action:

S=∫τ~iτ~fdτ~−gμνx˙μx˙ν,

defined on a manifold with metric gμν, which in the case of the Kerr, can be written in Boyer-Lindquist coordinates[1] as:

gμν=(rsrΣ−100−a2rsrsin2⁡θΣ0ΣΔ0000Σ0−a2rrssin2⁡θΣ00sin2⁡θ(a2rsrsin2⁡θΣ+a2+r2)),

where Σ=r2+a2cos⁡(θ)2 and Δ=r2−rsr+a2. In general, geodesics are constrained to satisfy the geodesic equations,

d2xμdτ~2=−Γμαβx˙αx˙β,

where,

Γμαβ=12gμν(gαν,β+gβν,α−gαβ,ν)

are the Christoffel symbols.

Hamilton-Jacobi Equations ​

The Kerr family of metrics belongs to the class of type-D (type-{2,2}) spacetimes. These spacetimes admit a Killing tensor from which it is possible to define a Carter constant and Penrose-Walker constant.[2] The Carter constant, in particular, allows solutions of the geodesic equations to be expressed in terms of quadratures through the Hamilton-Jacobi approach,[3] with equations:

H=12gμνpμpν,K=∂S∂τ~+H(x,∂S∂x,τ~)=0,pμ=∂S∂xμ.

The Carter constant is useful since it allows for seperable solutions to the Hamilton-Jacobi equations, resulting in a Hamiltonian principle function that takes the form,

S=∫RΔdr+∫Θdθ−Et+Lϕ+12κτ~

where κ is the conserved four momentum magnitude, E is the conserved energy, L is the conserved azimuthal angular momentum, and

R=Δ[−C+κr−(L−aE)2]+[E(r2+a2)−La]2,Θ=C+cos2⁡θ((κ+E2)a2−L2sin2⁡θ)

are the radial and inclination potentials that depend on the previously mentioned conserved quantities along with the Carter constant C.

Solutions by Quadrature ​

Null geodesics have κ=0; thus they are only dependent on the reduced Carter constant and angular momentum:

η=CE2λ=LE.

We use this fact to fix E=1 and write the radial and inclination potentials for null geodesics as,

R=−Δ[η+(λ−a)2]+[(r2+a2)−λa]2,Θ=η+cos2⁡θ(a2−λ2sin2⁡θ).

The Hamilton-Jacobi Equations then reduce to four first order differential equations:

Σr˙=drdτ=R,Σθ˙=dθdτ=Θ,Σϕ˙=dϕdτ=aΔ(r2+a2−aλ)+λsin2⁡θ−a,Σt˙=dtdτ=r2+a2Δ(r2+a2−aλ)+a(λ−asin2⁡θ),

where we have defined the Mino time by its relationship to the affine parameter; dτ=dτ~/Σ.

Equations (15)-(18) define a 'Kepler-like' problem for photons in the Kerr spacetime. The Mino time here acts similar to the Keplerian coordinate time in that problem, and can be expressed either in terms of r or θ using equations (15) and (16);

τ=τr(rf,ri)=∮drR, andτ=τθ(θf,θi)=∮dθΘ.

These equations are useful since τθ and τr can be inverted to write,

θi=τθ−1(τ,θf)ri=τr−1(τ,rf)

Thus for a given observer location, (tf,rf,θf,ϕf), and observer screen coordinate (α(λ,θf),β(λ,η,θf)), one can choose to parameterize the solutions of equations (15)-(18) by either Δτ,ri or θi.

Details on the solutions to equations (15)-(18) can be found in this review article by Gralla & Lupsasca[4].
